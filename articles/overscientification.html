The Over-Scientification of Trading

When Complexity Becomes Camouflage

Trading is hard. It’s messy. Uncertain. Emotional. So what do people do when they don’t really get it?

They complicate it.

They dress it up in complexity. They hide behind jargon. They start dropping words like “heteroskedasticity” and “stochastic dominance” — not because they understand them, but because you probably don’t.

This is the new con: sounding smart instead of being smart. Over-scientify everything, bury the truth in buzzwords, and pray no one asks you to explain it one layer up… or one layer down.

One-Level Traders

You’ve met them.

The “quant” who can recite what a Kalman filter does — but can’t tell you why or when it actually matters.

The guy who talks about statistical arbitrage — but is just mean-reverting between two garbage penny stocks with no business being paired.

Ask them to explain it simply? They can’t.
Ask them to go deeper? They short-circuit.
Because they don’t actually understand the thing. They just memorized the headline.

It’s trading cosplay. Science-flavored LARPing.

The Real Smart Ones? They Sound Simple

If you’ve ever listened to someone who actually understands markets, you’ll notice something weird:

They sound... simple.

They talk in terms of edge, time, risk, volatility, emotion — not in layers of unnecessary math. And when they do go technical, they tie it to something real. Not theory — context.

Because real knowledge can move in both directions:
It can zoom out and explain the big picture.
And it can zoom in and break down the mechanics.

Fake knowledge just sits in the middle — frozen in buzzword soup.

The Incentive to Sound Smart

This isn’t just a personality flaw. It’s a business model.

Over-complication sells. If you confuse people, you can sell them more:
- More indicators
- More courses
- More signals
- More hope

If you explain things clearly, they might realize how little edge you have. So the grift relies on making trading sound like quantum mechanics mixed with rocket science — with a sprinkle of Harvard lingo and ChatGPT-generated fluff.

That’s why some of the worst educators in finance are also the most technical-sounding.

Because when your edge is weak, your vocabulary has to be strong.

What Trading Actually Comes Down To

Forget the buzzwords. Forget the PDFs with Greek letters and PhD-level math. Trading — real trading — comes down to stuff you can explain to a 12-year-old:

  • Do you actually have an edge?
    Not "vibes," not "intuition." I mean: are you doing something that gives you a real chance to win more than you lose over time?
  • What are your odds?
    How often do you win? How much do you lose when you're wrong? Most people can't answer that — which means they're just gambling with extra steps.
  • How big are you betting?
    Bet too small and the win doesn’t matter. Bet too big and one loss kills you. Sizing isn't sexy, but it’s what keeps your account alive.
  • When do you admit you’re wrong?
    Every trade needs an “I’m out” point. If you don’t have that, you’re not trading — you’re just hoping.
  • Can you survive the randomness?
    You can do everything right and still lose. That’s the game. It’s not about being right once — it’s about staying in the game long enough to let the wins add up.

The Bottom Line

Markets don’t care how smart you sound. They care how well you survive.

If your strategy makes sense on paper but dies in live trading, guess what? It’s garbage. No matter how many acronyms are in your pitch deck.

So stop trying to sound smart. Start trying to be useful.
Because in this game, clarity is a better edge than complexity.

And when the market humbles you — and it will — big words won’t save you.

Truth will.
Risk management will.
Emotional control will.

Everything else is just noise with a thesaurus.
