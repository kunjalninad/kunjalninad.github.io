When Did Everything Become a Crisis?

Remember when people could joke, rant, or just be a little raw without the entire internet clutching its pearls? Yeah, me neither. Somewhere along the way, society decided that every word, every joke, every meme had to be sanitized so thoroughly it might as well be dipped in bleach.

What’s really wild is how quickly entire companies folded like cheap card tables the moment some Twitter mob got offended. The corporate cucks are out in full force, apologizing, deleting posts, firing people — all because some fragile soul got their feelings hurt.

What will you sell: the body or the soul?

Heck, calling someone a hoe is probably less of an insult today than calling them a corporate employee. Because selling your body? At least that’s honest. And the woman is not brain-washed into thinking that she loves the customer. Selling your soul to virtue signaling? Now that’s tragic. Because most people don't even know they're selling themselves, and now must convince themselves that this nonsense "means something."

But in corporate, it’s all fake smiles, endless apologies, and bending over backwards so far that they’re practically kissing their own backsides. And for what? To avoid a tweet storm? To dodge some Reddit keyboard warriors? It’s pathetic.

Why the Overcorrection is Killing Us

Look, I get it — some people say terrible stuff and need to be called out. But this culture of permanent outrage? It’s gone insane. You can’t say “merry Christmas” without starting a riot. You can’t crack a joke without a hundred people ready to cancel you faster than you can blink.

This hyper-policed speech environment isn’t freedom; it’s a cage. It’s making everyone boring, safe, and robotic. We’re trading actual conversation for endless political correctness, and the price is a society that’s less human and way more miserable.

Here’s a Thought

Maybe it’s time to stop treating adults like toddlers with bubble wrap emotions. Maybe we should grow a thicker skin, stop whining, and start having real conversations again — the messy, uncomfortable, sometimes offensive kind.

Otherwise, we’ll all end up in a world where every word is vetted by a committee of keyboard warriors and marketing departments. And honestly? That world sounds way worse than any “offensive” joke.
